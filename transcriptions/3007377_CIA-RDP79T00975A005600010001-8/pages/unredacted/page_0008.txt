Situation in the Congo

Gizengist troops have quit Luluabourg "like a wave on sand"--some disappeared into the surroundings and some withdrew.

The leaders of the invading force did not have control of their men, nor did the force have even the most meager logistical support. It evidently dispersed rather than submit to a disarmament agreement which its leaders negotiated with the local UN contingent. Gizengist officers involved in these negotiations and the pro-Mobutu Luluabourg garrison commander asked for and received UN protection. Most of those who dispersed were Luluas who had originally come from the area. All of the weapons they left behind were standard Congolese army items, none of Bloc manufacture.

Dayal reportedly told the Indonesian representative on 21 February that he planned to take "firm action" against Tshombé and Kalonji, "using the UNOC force ultimately to finish them." The representative in his message to Djakarta commented that with Tshombé, Kasavubu, and Ileo weakened by international reaction to the recent killings of Lumumba and his supporters, "a situation such as this can favor strong implementation of the Security Council by giving it the interpretation which we wish." He recommended that Indonesia align itself with other Afro-Asian nations, apparently to develop a line of action favoring Gizenga.

Belgium has promised $4,000,000 in the next two months for the purpose of paying the Leopoldville government's forces. In addition, an official British service reports that last December $40,000,000 was budgeted by Belgium for such projects as the payment of Belgian nationals working for the Congolese Government, scholarships, and educational institutions. However, the use to which almost three quarters of this sum was to be put is unknown.

The Sudanese ambassador in Moscow reported the Soviet Foreign Affairs Ministry made "daily inquiries" about the granting of transit visas
