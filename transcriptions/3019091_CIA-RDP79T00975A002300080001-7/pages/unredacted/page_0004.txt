1. CAFE FILHO ANNOUNCES PLANS TO RETURN TO
BRAZILIAN PRESIDENCY

Brazil's President Café Filho, who
had been on a leave of absence follow-
ing his heart attack on 3 November,
announced on 21 November that he was
returning to office. This announcement has created a new
political crisis, and army troops have again occupied key
positions in Rio de Janeiro.

Although Acting President Nereu Ramos
on 17 November reaffirmed Café's constitutional right to
return to his post, a large anti-Kubitschek newspaper as-
serted at the time that the army would intervene to prevent
such a move, and another prominent paper demanded that
Congress impeach Café if he returned to office. An abso-
lute majority in the Chamber of Deputies would suffice for
impeachment.

Café Filho has been consistently linked
with the important political and military forces averse to
the inauguration of President-elect Juscelino Kubitschek,
although after the 3 October elections he publicly declared
that Kubitschek, if validly elected, should take office.

Should Café Filho after resuming his
duties as president announce any sudden change in the mil-
itary members of his cabinet, War Minister Lott, who led
the army's "preventive coup" of 11 November, would prob-
ably oust Café in order "to preserve the constitution" and
permit the inauguration of Kubitschek as scheduled on
31 January.
