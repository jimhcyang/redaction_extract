GENERAL

1. New Delhi sees Moscow and Peiping at variance on Korean truce:

Ambassador Bowles has found that Prime
Minister Nehru, Indian Foreign Office
officials, and most chiefs of diplomatic
missions in New Delhi believe that the
Soviet Union and Communist China have
not seen "eye to eye" on the Korean truce question.

The Indian officials maintain that Peiping
led them to believe that their truce plan "might" be acceptable. The
violence of Vyshinsky's attack on their plan is construed as Soviet
pressure to prevent Peiping's acceptance.

Comment: As far as is known, Peiping's
encouragement of the Indian proposal amounted to no more than an
indication that removal of the POW's from American control would
be a step in the right direction. Soviet and Satellite spokesmen at
the UN were hinting at the same time that a compromise was possible.
Chinese Communist Premier Chou En-lai
echoed Vyshinsky's rejection of the Indian plan, and there is still no
evidence of Sino-Soviet disagreement on Korean truce issues.
