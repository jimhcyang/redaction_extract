Afghanistan-Pakistan: Relations between Afghanistan and Pakistan are growing worse as Rawalpindi follows through on its decision to take a harder line with Kabul. Pakistan's ambassador in Kabul says he was "really rough" on Afghan Foreign Minister Naim during a 16 February meeting, warning him that Afghanistan must change its unfriendly policy regarding Pushtoonistan. Radio Pakistan on the same day declared, "No Afghan can lead a decent life as long as these rulers of Kabul control his destiny." Pakistani Foreign Minister Qadir, until now a moderating influence, has apparently become convinced that this new line will induce Kabul to alter its policy. The Afghans, who may believe the United States supports Pakistan's hardened attitude, appear to be looking for ways to hit back at the Pakistanis.

III. THE WEST
Cuba: Che Guevara, probably the most strongly anti-American and pro-Communist leader in the Castro regime, will be replaced as head of the National Bank. Guevara is believed to have caused resentment among Castro followers by his open exercise of power in numerous sectors of the government. Fidel Castro has become jealous of Guevara and in order to get him out of Cuba will send him to Moscow as ambassador when diplomatic relations are resumed.

Dominican Republic: A large public "homage" to Generalissimo Trujillo being organized by the regime for 21 February as a show of strength could, in the present climate of unrest, provoke violence. Dissidents, who include representatives of some of the most prominent Dominican families, are so embittered that they are becoming reckless. Arrests and police excesses continue and the economic situation is growing worse. (Page 6)
