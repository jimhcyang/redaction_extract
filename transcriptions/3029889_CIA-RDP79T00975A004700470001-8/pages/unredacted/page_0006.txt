I. THE COMMUNIST BLOC

Ulbricht-Khrushchev Relations Reportedly Strained

East German attitudes favoring Communist China appear to be straining relations between Khrushchev and East Germany. The Soviet leader is annoyed with Ulbricht, especially because of a telegram--the contents of which are not known--he sent to Mao Tse-tung in answer to the latter's greeting on the occasion of East Germany's tenth anniversary.

Other East German actions may have contributed to Khrushchev's misgivings. For example, East German politburo member Hermann Matern, at Peiping's tenth anniversary celebrations, commented favorably on the communes, and Premier Grotewohl in late September publicly backed the position taken by Peiping rather than taking the neutral Soviet view on the Chinese-Indian border dispute.

Khrushchev may also have been irritated by the raising of the new East German flag over elevated rail stations in West Berlin, which was a unilateral East German action. A low-level functionary had ordered the flag-raising and the strong reaction to this incident made repetition unlikely.

Ulbricht is also reported to believe, despite assurances from Khrushchev, that the Soviet leader may have been too conciliatory during his meeting with President Eisenhower. Ulbricht fears that more than just tactical concessions were made at the expense of his position and of East German prestige. He plans to go to Moscow soon for talks with Khrushchev.
