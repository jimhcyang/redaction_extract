1. LAOS PRESSED FOR DIPLOMATIC EXCHANGES WITH USSR AND POLAND

The USSR and Poland are now pressing the Laotian government to exchange [[PRED_REDACTION_4]]diplomatic[[/PRED_REDACTION_4]] representatives on the basis of negotiations undertaken by Premier Souvanna Phouma during his earlier tenure, according to Foreign Minister Phoui Sananikone.

Souvanna has been negotiating with both countries for "some months," according to Phoui, and the Laotian government has recently received Soviet [[PRED_REDACTION_5]]agrement[[/PRED_REDACTION_5]] for the appointment of its ambassador in France to be [[PRED_REDACTION_6]]concurrently[[/PRED_REDACTION_6]] ambassador to the Soviet Union, as requested by Souvanna over a year ago. In addition, the Polish truce [[PRED_REDACTION_7]]commissioner[[/PRED_REDACTION_7]] has presented a letter from his government, [[PRED_REDACTION_8]]ostensibly[[/PRED_REDACTION_8]] in line with an earlier request by Souvanna, granting de facto recognition to Laos and has suggested the way is now clear for an exchange of envoys.

Phoui states he had been completely [[PRED_REDACTION_9]]uninformed[[/PRED_REDACTION_9]] on both these moves of Souvanna, and now is in a quandary over the situation.

Comment

Last November, as a result of Soviet [[PRED_REDACTION_10]]action[[/PRED_REDACTION_10]] in Hungary, the Laotian cabinet [[PRED_REDACTION_11]]reversed[[/PRED_REDACTION_11]] a decision to exchange representatives with the USSR.

Souvanna may have sought an exchange of representatives with bloc countries in the hope of improving the climate of negotiations with the Pathet Lao, who have [[PRED_REDACTION_12]]insisted[[/PRED_REDACTION_12]] on a neutral foreign policy. In his earlier tenure, [[PRED_REDACTION_13]]however[[/PRED_REDACTION_13]], Souvanna had a freer hand in determining Laotian policy; Phoui and Interior Minister Katay, leading anti-Communists of the two-party alliance which formed the present government, are now consolidating their power in the national assembly.

2. HATTA'S VIEWS ON COMMUNIST THREAT TO INDONESIA

Former vice president Hatta told [[PRED_REDACTION_14]]Ambassador[[/PRED_REDACTION_14]] Allison on 31 August that he saw no danger of a Communist [[PRED_REDACTION_15]]takeover[[/PRED_REDACTION_15]] in Indonesia in the immediate [[PRED_REDACTION_16]]future[[/PRED_REDACTION_16]]. In support of this view, he pointed to the Communists' present minority in parliament, the generally [[PRED_REDACTION_17]]anti-Communist[[/PRED_REDACTION_17]] attitude of the army, and the lack of vigorous Communist leaders.

Hatta felt, however, that in the long run the Communist threat could become critical unless there was improvement in the economic situation in central and east Java, and unless leaders of the National party became convinced of the danger of cooperation with the Communists.

Hatta apparently believes that the best that can be expected of the national round-table conference starting on 10 September in Djakarta is an improvement in relations between dissident leaders and the government to the point that additional time will be permitted to work out their disagreements.

Comment

Recent Communist party gains in local Java elections indicate that it enjoys effective direction even though none of its leaders is a [[PRED_REDACTION_18]]dynamic[[/PRED_REDACTION_18]] public figure.

Some National party leaders reportedly favor ending the party's cooperation with the Communists, but a split apparently is continuing between the older leaders, who developed the Communist association, and younger [[PRED_REDACTION_19]]leaders[[/PRED_REDACTION_19]]. The economic situation in Java is rapidly [[PRED_REDACTION_20]]deteriorating[[/PRED_REDACTION_20]] without much hope that this trend will be reversed.

3. COMMUNIST-INSPIRED GENERAL STRIKE REPORTEDLY PLANNED IN ECUADOR

[[PRED_REDACTION_21]]Comment on[[/PRED_REDACTION_21]]:

A Communist-inspired general strike in Ecuador, reportedly planned for early September, could paralyze the Ecuadoran economy and challenge the stability of President Ponce's Conservative regime.

[[PRED_REDACTION_22]]American-owned Manabi Exploration Company--Ecuador's second largest oil producer--has advised the American embassy in Quito that Communists are behind "impossible" demands for a new union contract and will strike his company as a means of launching a general strike to embarrass Ponce. The company's planned dismissal of 100 workers about 4 September is likely to precipitate the movement[[/PRED_REDACTION_22]].

The Communist-oriented oil workers' federation is affiliated with the Communist-oriented [[PRED_REDACTION_23]]Confederation[[/PRED_REDACTION_23]] of Ecuadoran Workers (CTE), Ecuador's leading labor federation, which in turn is an affiliate of the [[PRED_REDACTION_24]]Communist[[/PRED_REDACTION_24]] Confederation of Latin American Workers and World Federation of Trade Unions. Staunchly anti-Ponce, the CTE might endorse a general strike and its [[PRED_REDACTION_25]]Communist-penetrated[[/PRED_REDACTION_25]] railway union affiliate would probably support it, because the government has recently discharged 2,000 railway workers.

The government, however, has been alerted to the potential danger of the strike and has [[PRED_REDACTION_26]]promised[[/PRED_REDACTION_26]] to apply force if necessary to back the American [[PRED_REDACTION_27]]company's[[/PRED_REDACTION_27]] dismissals.

4. AUSTRIAN CHANCELLOR CAUTIOUS ON PROPOSED TRIP TO MOSCOW

Austrian Chancellor Julius Raab told the American embassy in Vienna on 29 August that for a variety of reasons he now thinks he will postpone until early next year his prospective visit to Moscow to discuss a scaling down of Austrian reparations deliveries. He implied that Moscow's informal offer to engage in [[PRED_REDACTION_28]]confidential[[/PRED_REDACTION_28]] personal negotiations would not justify an official visit in Austrian eyes, and added that, in any case, internal [[PRED_REDACTION_29]]political[[/PRED_REDACTION_29]] problems would preclude his making the trip this fall. He expressed a belief that the present governmental situation in Moscow is not sufficiently stable.

The chancellor remarked as an [[PRED_REDACTION_30]]afterthought[[/PRED_REDACTION_30]] and with evident dissatisfaction that Soviet [[PRED_REDACTION_31]]Ambassador[[/PRED_REDACTION_31]] Lapin had recently had his first visit with Socialist Vice Chancellor Pittermann.

[[PRED_REDACTION_32]]Comment

Raab was reported on 31 August to have suffered a mild stroke or heart attack which may restrict his traveling[[/PRED_REDACTION_32]].

In any case, he is apparently worried that acceptance of Moscow's offer to negotiate with him [[PRED_REDACTION_33]]personally[[/PRED_REDACTION_33]] on a 50-percent reduction in reparations might [[PRED_REDACTION_34]]revive[[/PRED_REDACTION_34]] suspicions that he is incautious in dealing with the [[PRED_REDACTION_35]]Soviet[[/PRED_REDACTION_35]] Union. His distress over the Soviet ambassador's [[PRED_REDACTION_36]]approach[[/PRED_REDACTION_36]] to Pittermann again suggests that Raab's interest in the negotiations is motivated in part by a desire to claim [[PRED_REDACTION_37]]partisan[[/PRED_REDACTION_37]] credit for any concessions gained.
