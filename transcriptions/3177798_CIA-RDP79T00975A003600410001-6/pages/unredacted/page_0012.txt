II. ASIA-AFRICA

Possible Unrest Among Saudi Arabian Oil Workers

Indications of possible labor unrest in the Eastern Province of Saudi Arabia, locus of the American-owned oil operations, have been observed in recent weeks. Rumors have spread through at least one Aramco labor camp that many workers will not return to work after the holidays following the end of the Moslem holy month of Ramadan on 19 April. An open letter recently posted at Ras Tanura called on Saudi contractors to cease "exploiting laborers in this shameful way," and demanded higher pay, better housing, vacations, medical treatment, and improved transportation to and from work. During the night signs were painted on walls in Dammam calling for overthrow of the monarchy.

Labor unrest at this time may have been caused in part by the Saudi economic crisis in which the dearth of foreign exchange has resulted in a shortage of consumer goods and higher food prices. Hopes for significant internal reform following Crown Prince Faysal's assumption of broad powers on 22 March may have led the working populace to believe it could agitate successfully, although demonstrations and strike efforts by laborers in the past have been brutally suppressed by the provincial governor, Saud ibn Jiluwi. Agitation for social and political reform, and against the American position in the Eastern Province, is also exerted among the working force by the many Palestinian Arab workers in the area. Labor unrest could be exploited by the Saudi authorities to seek new concessions from Aramco.
