1. RIOTS IN TAIPEI

[[PRED_REDACTION_4]]Comment on[[/PRED_REDACTION_4]]:

The mob violence in Taipei on [[PRED_REDACTION_5]]21[[/PRED_REDACTION_5]] May
which resulted in the wrecking of the US
embassy and the US Information Agency
building reveals strong and widespread
anti-American sentiment on Taiwan. The
rioting lasted for 12 hours and eased only
after heavily armed Chinese troops [[PRED_REDACTION_6]]occu-
pied[[/PRED_REDACTION_6]] the city.

The riots were inspired by inflammatory
articles in the local newspapers which [[PRED_REDACTION_7]]bit-
terly[[/PRED_REDACTION_7]] assailed the US military court [[PRED_REDACTION_8]]ac-
quittal[[/PRED_REDACTION_8]] of a MAAG sergeant who shot and
killed a Chinese "peeping Tom." The [[PRED_REDACTION_9]]Na-
tionalist[[/PRED_REDACTION_9]] government had protested the court
decision shortly before the disorders began.
National Youth Corps students appear to
have participated in the rioting in an organized manner. [[PRED_REDACTION_10]]Am-
bassador[[/PRED_REDACTION_10]] Rankin, in a cable sent immediately after he returned
to Taipei from a trip to Hong Kong, reported "various, if
fragmentary, indications that the riots were organized in [[PRED_REDACTION_11]]ad-
vance[[/PRED_REDACTION_11]]."

Negotiations for a status-of-forces [[PRED_REDACTION_12]]agree-
ment[[/PRED_REDACTION_12]] between the United States and the Nationalist government,
which have been stalled for some months, now may become even
more difficult.

Chinese Communist broadcasts have recently
taken the line that the United States is making a "colony" of
Taiwan. Pro-Communist newspapers in Hong Kong have [[PRED_REDACTION_13]]al-
ready[[/PRED_REDACTION_13]] denounced the acquittal verdict and Peiping can be [[PRED_REDACTION_14]]ex-
pected[[/PRED_REDACTION_14]] to exploit the riots with propaganda calling for "Chinese
unity" against American "imperialism."

2. LIBYAN PRIME MINISTER RESIGNS

The British financial adviser to King
Idriss of Libya has informed the [[PRED_REDACTION_16]]Amer-
ican[[/PRED_REDACTION_16]] ambassador that the resignation of
Prime Minister Mustafa Ben Halim was
accepted on 24 May. Former deputy prime minister and
minister of foreign affairs Abdul Majid Kubaar has been
asked to form a new cabinet.

Ben Halim will be officially named as
an adviser to the new prime minister, as will Mahmud [[PRED_REDACTION_17]]Mun-
tasser[[/PRED_REDACTION_17]], former prime minister and present ambassador to
London. Ali Sahli and Muhyi al-Din Fikini, ministers of
communications and finance, respectively, have been ousted.
Defense Minister Abdul Qadir al-Allam will be in charge of
communications, and the defense post will be filled "for a
short while" by Saddiq Muntasser, former ambassador to
Egypt.

The new cabinet reportedly will be sworn
in on 26 May and is expected to retain the pro-Western [[PRED_REDACTION_18]]orien-
tation[[/PRED_REDACTION_18]] of its predecessor. The changes are said to be [[PRED_REDACTION_19]]com-
pletely[[/PRED_REDACTION_19]] satisfactory to Ben Halim.

Comment

Ben Halim was strongly pro-Western [[PRED_REDACTION_20]]dur-
ing[[/PRED_REDACTION_20]] the latter part of his three-year tenure
as prime minister. Prime Minister-designate Kubaar was
personally chosen last March by Ben Halim as his deputy [[PRED_REDACTION_21]]be-
cause[[/PRED_REDACTION_21]] of his "loyalty and reliability." The new defense [[PRED_REDACTION_22]]min-
ister[[/PRED_REDACTION_22]], Saddiq Muntasser, is pro-Egyptian and a foe of Ben
Halim, but the ousting of Sahli and Fikini, who had been [[PRED_REDACTION_23]]op-
posed[[/PRED_REDACTION_23]] to the policies of Ben Halim, would seem to support the
view that the new government will continue to pursue generally
pro-Western policies.

3. MASJUMI LEADER ATTACKS INDONESIAN PRESIDENT
AND CABINET

Former premier Harahap, the Masjumi
parliamentary leader, during debates on
the Indonesian cabinet's program on 21
May charged that President Sukarno had
acted irresponsibly in declaring martial
law, had violated the constitution by appointing the cabinet
himself, and had demolished every form of democracy. He
also accused the cabinet of violating the constitution when
it created the "national advisory council" by an emergency
cabinet decree.

The spokesman of the [[PRED_REDACTION_25]]Nahdlatul[[/PRED_REDACTION_25]] Ulama
(NU) Party, however, stated that his party's position would
depend on future actions of the cabinet, and that the NU did
not reject the national council out of hand.

Comment

The lack of unity between the two major
Moslem parties will keep parliament [[PRED_REDACTION_26]]in-
effective[[/PRED_REDACTION_26]] as an instrument of opposition to Sukarno and the
cabinet. Parliament's influence over the government's [[PRED_REDACTION_27]]pro-
gram[[/PRED_REDACTION_27]] has already been circumscribed by Prime Minister
Djuanda's announcement that parliament could debate the
cabinet's program as much as it pleased, but would not be
asked for a vote of confidence in the government.

Harahap's attack on Sukarno, probably
the most violent public criticism of the president by an
Indonesian spokesman to date, is likely further to [[PRED_REDACTION_28]]compli-
cate[[/PRED_REDACTION_28]] relations between Djakarta and the dissident provinces
which are Masjumi strongholds.

4. YUGOSLAV DEFENSE MINISTER TO VISIT MOSCOW

The trip of Yugoslav defense minister
Gosnjak to Moscow scheduled for June
is a "purely courtesy one," according
to a Yugoslav Foreign Ministry official,
Franc Kos. He told an American embassy official on 23
May that the trip had been arranged in principle at the time
of Tito's trip to Moscow last June. Kos, citing recent Soviet
expressions of hostility, stated that "relations between [[PRED_REDACTION_30]]Bel-
grade[[/PRED_REDACTION_30]] and Moscow were hardly conducive to friendliness
and certainly not to obtaining anything concrete in the way
of military assistance."

Asked what Tito had in mind when he stated
early this week that relations are improving, Kos replied that
Tito undoubtedly had in mind not the USSR, but Poland, Rumania,
and even Czechoslovakia, whose Premier Siroky would [[PRED_REDACTION_31]]proba-
bly[[/PRED_REDACTION_31]] visit Belgrade in June.

Comment

Tito probably timed Gosnjak's trip to [[PRED_REDACTION_32]]dem-
onstrate[[/PRED_REDACTION_32]] Yugoslavia's independence [[PRED_REDACTION_33]]follow-
ing[[/PRED_REDACTION_33]] the publicly announced decision by the United States to [[PRED_REDACTION_34]]re-
sume[[/PRED_REDACTION_34]] military aid deliveries. He probably now feels it useful
to demonstrate his willingness to call a truce in the [[PRED_REDACTION_35]]ideologi-
cal[[/PRED_REDACTION_35]] war with Moscow. This will be the first high-level [[PRED_REDACTION_36]]Yugoslav-
Soviet[[/PRED_REDACTION_36]] contact since October.
