FAR EAST

1. Rightist assassination attempt against Yoshida reported:

Prime Minister Yoshida's daughter has told Ambassador Allison that a rightist attempt was made on 4 May to assassinate six persons, including her father and herself. The press has played down the incident, reporting only that "a seemingly insane young man" was arrested for invading the prime minister's home.

Comment: According to a Japanese news agency, there have been other attempts against Yoshida recently. In March police authorities reportedly gave additional protection to top government and Liberal Party leaders as a precaution against rightist violence in protest against the current political scandals.

2. Soviet-trained Korean named North Korean minister of agriculture:

Pyongyang radio on 1 May referred to Kim Il as "vice premier and concurrently minister of agriculture" of the North Korean cabinet. Kim gave the major address at the May Day celebration in Pyongyang, which was attended by Premier Kim Il-sung and other high party and government leaders.

Comment: The appointment of Kim Il, who was born and educated in the Soviet Union, to the important Agricultural Ministry provides new evidence of Soviet control over the North Korean regime.

Kim is a trusted underling of Kim Il-sung and was used by the regime after 1945 to forestall control of the army by the Chinese-oriented faction of Korean Communists under General Mu Chong. Since last year's purge Kim Il has risen rapidly in the party and the government, having been named to the key central political committee last August and to a vice premiership on 25 March. He is currently listed as the third-ranking vice premier.
