Gizenga, asked Khrushchev, Nasir, Guinea's President Touré, Mali's President Keita, and Morocco's King Mohamed V to "issue an ultimatum for confirmation of the lives" of Lumumba and his associates, adding that "your direct action for helping us is more than ever recommended."

Gizenga chastised his representative in Cairo for not providing adequate warning that the aid which had been expected would not arrive. The representative was told to ask "our Soviet comrades" to request the UN "vigorously" for permission "to bring us what we need to keep alive." Gizenga said that in the event this permission was denied, he would "undertake measures which will make the entire earth shudder." He alluded to the Stanleyville military forces' increasing impatience over the inability of their supporters abroad to send in "aid in the form of arms and ammunition."
