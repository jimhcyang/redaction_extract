2. NEW JORDANIAN CABINET

The composition of the new Jordanian
cabinet appointed on 8 January makes
Jordanian adherence to the Baghdad
pact even more unlikely. Prime Min-
ister Samir al-Rifai has publicly stated that Jordan will
not adhere to the pact, and the new foreign minister,
Hussain al-Khalidi, is an anti-British nationalist and re-
portedly has favored Jordan's alliance with the Egyptian-
Syrian-Saudi Arabian axis.

While the make-up of the new cabinet
should appease the antipact elements who sparked the rioting,
other associated elements, including the Communists, may
seek to continue demonstrations with a view to obtaining a
renewal of the promise of new elections.

The American embassy in Amman be-
lieves that a test is approaching as to whether moderate
politicians and the Arab Legion can control the country or
whether extremists will take over. The embassy believes
that the extremists will not succeed in the near future, but
that disorders will continue. Local British officials, how-
ever, are alarmed to the extent that they are considering
evacuation to the British-controlled air base outside Amman.

An important factor in the immediate fu-
ture will be the attitude of Saudi Arabia. A number of the new
cabinet members, including the prime minister, have enjoyed
close relations and financial aid from the Saudis. Having ob-
tained a government opposed to the Baghdad pact, Saudi Arabia
may now throw the weight of its influence on the side of sta-
bility.
