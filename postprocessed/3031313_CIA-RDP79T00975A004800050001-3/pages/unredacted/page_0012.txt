III. THE WEST

Austria Calls Tirol Dispute With Italy "Dangerous and Explosive"

Austrian Foreign Minister Bruno Kreisky has told American Embassy officials in Vienna that he is very discouraged over the course of the South Tirol dispute with Italy which he characterized as Vienna's "most important foreign-policy question." The Italians have already rejected Austrian proposals for South Tirolean autonomy under Italian sovereignty, according to Kreisky, who is being pressed by South Tirolean leaders to seek instead full self-determination--presumably through a plebiscite, a demand he has so far refused to support. He says that the Italian Government does not appreciate the "dangerous and explosive nature" of the situation in the area, and that current bilateral talks are getting nowhere.

The Austrian foreign minister is hoping to persuade Washington to mediate the dispute and has cited the solution of the Trieste problem as the kind of approach he has in mind. Failing this, he is apparently determined to attempt to place the issue before the UN General Assembly. Although he has publicly denied putting a time limit on the bilateral talks with Italy, he has also said he would "wait until the end of the year." Despite some sniping at his tactics, Kreisky has wide popular support in Austria. Both his prestige and that of the Socialist party are increasingly committed to achieving a South Tirol "solution." The People's party is going along with the Socialists in making the issue a "common coalition matter."

Rome apparently intends to insist on full support from its NATO partners.
