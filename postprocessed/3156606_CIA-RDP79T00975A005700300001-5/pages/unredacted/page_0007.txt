South Korea: Constitutional and administrative actions of the South Korean regime suggest that the leadership envisages an extended period of military rule. According to press reports, the present constitution has been suspended, and Foreign Minister Kim Hong-il told the American chargé on 27 May that a provisional constitution was being drafted to legalize the reforms that have been made, principally the transfer of the powers of the now dissolved National Assembly to the Supreme Council for National Reconstruction. The council's proposal to submit the constitution to a popular referendum appears to be designed to develop popular support. The council's reported plan to appoint military personnel to administrative positions at all levels of local government, including that of county chief, is almost certainly an effort toward long-term entrenchment in power.

Iran: Prime Minister Amini has said that he will demand greater authority from the Shah or will resign, since he believes far-reaching reforms are impossible in Iran as long as the Shah retains ultimate authority for himself. Amini fears that the Shah intends to play former security chief General Bakhtiar against him in an effort to perpetuate the monarch's position. the prime minister and his brother, retired General Mahmud Amini, are working on a "cooperative venture" to strengthen the prime minister's position.
