DAILY BRIEF

*Laos. Separate political and military talks between the royal government, the Pathet Lao, and the Souvanna Phouma group began at Namone on 29 May, following agreement to establish a military subcommittee. At the apparent insistence of the Pathet Lao, the "duties and policies" of a coalition government will be taken up first in the political talks, beginning with the 31 May session. Discussion of this item of the agenda is likely to be time consuming and will offer the Pathet Lao full scope for raising extraneous issues and for using the talks as a propaganda vehicle. For the parallel military talks, a four-point agenda was adopted relating to maintenance of the cease-fire and collaboration with the ICC.

The military situation remains generally quiet, although scattered guerrilla activity continues to be reported. The lull in the fighting in the Pa Dong area, apparently stemming from temporary depletion of the Communists' artillery ammunition stocks, persisted through 29 May. Souvanna and Souphannouvong are reported to have voiced no objection to the proposed ICC inspection of the Pa Dong front during a recent interview with ICC Chairman Sen in Xieng Khouang; however, they stated that they would have to "study" the matter before giving a formal reply, a tactic calculated to give their forces additional time in which to capture Pa Dong.

Bloc airlift operations continue to be scheduled through 30 May. (Map[[/PRED_REDACTION_7]])

South Korea: Constitutional and administrative actions of the South Korean regime suggest that the leadership envisages an extended period of military rule. According to press reports, the present constitution has been suspended, and Foreign Minister Kim [[PRED_REDACTION_9]]Hong-il[[/PRED_REDACTION_9]] told the American [[PRED_REDACTION_10]]charg[[/PRED_REDACTION_10]]é on 27 May that a provisional constitution was being drafted to legalize the reforms that have been made, principally the transfer of the powers of the now dissolved National Assembly to the Supreme Council for National Reconstruction. The council's proposal to submit the constitution to a popular referendum appears to be designed to develop popular support. The council's reported plan to appoint military personnel to administrative positions at all levels of local government, including that of county chief, is almost certainly an effort toward long-term entrenchment in power.

[[PRED_REDACTION_11]]Iran: Prime Minister Amini has said that he will demand greater authority from the Shah or will resign, since he believes far-reaching reforms are impossible in Iran as long as the Shah retains ultimate authority for himself. Amini fears that the Shah intends to play former security chief General Bakhtiar against him in an effort to perpetuate the monarch's position. the prime minister and his brother, retired General Mahmud Amini, are working on a "cooperative venture" to strengthen the prime minister's position.

Amini is reported anxious to reach an understanding with the National Front.

President Kasavubu is expected to request UN guarantees for the safety of deputies being invited to Leopoldville from other areas. USSR: The shake-up in the Soviet bureaucracy which began in late 1960 and which Khrushchev accelerated this year in an effort to bolster lagging agricultural production and purge the party and government bureaucracies of corrupt and incompetent officials shows no signs of diminishing. As a result of the current campaign and earlier attrition, the turnover in the membership of the central committee elected at the 22nd party congress in October will certainly be greater than occurred in 1956, when 35 percent were replaced; current indications point to a turnover of almost 50 percent. Further high-level changes are probably in the offing.

[[PRED_REDACTION_13]]USSR-China: Communist China has sold gold in Paris, using the USSR as an agent. On 17 and 18 May the USSR credited about $11,000,000 and $3,000,000 respectively in sterling to China's account in Moscow, amounts which evidently represent the receipts from gold sales consummated in France on two preceding days. West Germany: The announcement that the Social Democratic party (SPD) is planning a meeting of SPD Bundestag deputies in West Berlin on 6 June will probably be attacked by the bloc as an example of the type of "provocation" which makes a Berlin settlement urgent. This action is planned by SPD leaders in lieu of a plenary session of the Bundestag which Chancellor Adenauer opposes because he feels it would increase East-West tensions [[PRED_REDACTION_15]]and focus attention on Mayor Brandt, his opponent in the September national elections. Brandt reportedly believes that even a meeting of the SPD Bundestag members will reassert the right to meet in West Berlin and also will put Adenauer's Christian Democrats in the position of showing a lack of sympathy for Berlin[[/PRED_REDACTION_15]].

Turkey: The 27 May anniversary of the Turkish military coup passed without incident, and the ruling Committee of National Union (CNU) used the occasion for final Assembly approval of the new constitution, another step in the gradual return to civil government. Chief of State General Gursel has again reaffirmed the regime's intention to hold popular elections and organize the new parliament no later than October 1961. The previously reported arrests which took place in mid-May for plotting against the military regime apparently resulted from over-sensitivity of a few members of the regime rather than from any serious threat to the government. Meanwhile, the trials of political prisoners from the ousted Bayar-Menderes regime, now in their eighth month, are likely to be completed by mid-July. Some of the leaders of the previous government will probably be sentenced to death.
