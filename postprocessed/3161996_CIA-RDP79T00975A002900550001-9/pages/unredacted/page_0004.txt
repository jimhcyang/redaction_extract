1. USSR EXPECTS NEGATIVE WESTERN RESPONSE TO
ITS MIDDLE EAST PROPOSALS

Soviet deputy foreign minister Zorin told Egyptian ambassador Kuni on 15 February that the six proposals contained in the Soviet note of 11 February should be "taken together as a whole" and that Western maneuvers to modify or reject some of them would be unacceptable. Zorin predicted that a complete negative attitude, "which is more probable," would be very difficult for the West to justify.

In requesting closer liaison between the USSR and Egypt, Zorin complained that neither Moscow, its UN delegates nor the Soviet ambassador in Cairo was familiar with developments in Egypt, the main line of Egyptian policy, or the information necessary to be of help.

Zorin warned that the introduction of international forces into the area constituted a great danger. He added that Hammarskjold was influenced by "his New York atmosphere" and that "we are dealing with a system through which America acts as a real force."
