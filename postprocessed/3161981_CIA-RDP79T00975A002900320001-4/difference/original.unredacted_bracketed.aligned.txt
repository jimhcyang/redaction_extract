1. POUJADIST DISORDERS POSSIBLE IN 27 JANUARY
PARIS BY-ELECTION

[[PRED_REDACTION_4]]Comment on[[/PRED_REDACTION_4]]:

Antitax leader Pierre Poujade's [[PRED_REDACTION_5]]candidacy[[/PRED_REDACTION_5]] for the 27 January run-off ballot in the Seine district raises the [[PRED_REDACTION_6]]possibility[[/PRED_REDACTION_6]] of further disturbances by [[PRED_REDACTION_7]]ultranationalist[[/PRED_REDACTION_7]] groups. The Poujadists [[PRED_REDACTION_8]]successfully[[/PRED_REDACTION_8]] used strong-arm tactics to break up a [[PRED_REDACTION_9]]Mendes-France[[/PRED_REDACTION_9]] Radical rally before the first ballot, but they are likely to meet strong competition from the Communists, who can be expected to instigate their own disturbances.

Poujade, who has already condemned the Fourth Republic and demanded power in the name of his movement, did not run for election in 1956 but is seeking an assembly seat now ostensibly to defend the French army and particularly General Jacques Faure, involved with [[PRED_REDACTION_10]]conspiratorial[[/PRED_REDACTION_10]] settlers in Algeria. Poujade is probably mainly [[PRED_REDACTION_11]]interested[[/PRED_REDACTION_11]] in enhancing his own waning power and restoring [[PRED_REDACTION_12]]cohesion[[/PRED_REDACTION_12]] to his organization, which has split badly since the assembly elections in January 1956. The extent of support from his own group may become apparent following the [[PRED_REDACTION_13]]tentatively[[/PRED_REDACTION_13]] scheduled rally this Thursday; hundreds of his [[PRED_REDACTION_14]]followers[[/PRED_REDACTION_14]] are reported to be flocking to Paris. However, Jean Le Pen, a Poujadist deputy from the Seine district, who is particularly influential among the students, is reportedly at odds with Poujade.

Since no candidate won a majority in the 13 January vote, a plurality will be sufficient to elect a [[PRED_REDACTION_15]]candidate[[/PRED_REDACTION_15]] on the second ballot. Conservative candidate Tardieu was the front runner, while the Communist vote dropped [[PRED_REDACTION_16]]sharply[[/PRED_REDACTION_16]]. Poujade's candidacy may jeopardize Tardieu's chances and improve the prospects for a Communist comeback.

2. APPOINTMENT OF NEW POLISH AMBASSADOR
TO MOSCOW

[[PRED_REDACTION_17]]Comment on[[/PRED_REDACTION_17]]:

Poland's appointment of Tadeusz Gede to replace Waclaw Lewikowski as [[PRED_REDACTION_18]]ambassador[[/PRED_REDACTION_18]] to the Kremlin, announced on 21 [[PRED_REDACTION_19]]January[[/PRED_REDACTION_19]], probably heralds a new series of shifts designed to place men that Gomulka can trust in key positions. Earlier reports stated that [[PRED_REDACTION_20]]Ambassador[[/PRED_REDACTION_20]] Spasowski in Washington would also be replaced.

Gede, whose post as deputy premier was eliminated in the streamlining of the government following Gomulka's return to power in October 1956, was minister of foreign trade from 1949 to 1952. Gomulka is probably [[PRED_REDACTION_21]]drawing[[/PRED_REDACTION_21]] on Gede's experience in Orbit trade matters to ensure [[PRED_REDACTION_22]]expert[[/PRED_REDACTION_22]] handling of Poland's vital economic relations with the Soviet Union.

Lewikowski was a politburo member of the wartime Polish Communist Party. He served as a deputy [[PRED_REDACTION_23]]minister[[/PRED_REDACTION_23]] of security before his appointment to Moscow in 1953.

[[PRED_REDACTION_24]]3. PLANS FOR FUTURE CONSERVATIVE COALITION IN
SOUTH KOREA

Yi Ki-pung, speaker of South Korea's National Assembly and leader of the Liberal Party, plans to approach opposition Democratic Party leader Cho Pyong-ok concerning the formation of a coalition government to avert chaos following Rhee's death, according to a Korean newspaperman close to Yi.

Yi's plan reportedly is to allow Vice President Chang Myon, a member of the Democratic Party, to succeed to the presidency, but to put through a constitutional amendment providing for a parliamentary form of government which would make the president a figurehead. Cho Pyong-ok would then become prime minister--a post which was abolished by Rhee.

Comment

The passing of this information to an American official suggests that Yi may desire to ascertain the United States' reaction. Yi's plan reflects indications that the Democratic Party will emerge as the dominant conservative party in South Korea following Rhee's death and the expected disintegration of the Liberal Party.

Cho, at one time Seoul police chief and home minister under Rhee, has emerged as one of Rhee's most able and vigorous opponents[[/PRED_REDACTION_24]].

4. RENEWAL OF US-RUMANIAN TENSION FORESEEN

[[PRED_REDACTION_25]]Comment on[[/PRED_REDACTION_25]]:

Official confirmation on 19 January of earlier hints that American observers would not be allowed at the Rumanian national elections on 3 February, [[PRED_REDACTION_26]]coupled[[/PRED_REDACTION_26]] with other recent anti-American moves, is interpreted by the American minister in [[PRED_REDACTION_27]]Bucharest[[/PRED_REDACTION_27]] as introducing a period of renewed tension in [[PRED_REDACTION_28]]US-Rumanian[[/PRED_REDACTION_28]] relations.

Recent manifestations of a stiffened line toward the US include strong press attacks, hints at future restrictions on the legation's use of a USAF plane for travel out of the country, and greatly increased [[PRED_REDACTION_29]]Rumanian[[/PRED_REDACTION_29]] secret police attentiveness to legation activities.

In conformity with the general [[PRED_REDACTION_30]]Communist[[/PRED_REDACTION_30]] line, however, the Rumanian government appears to be attempting to keep cultural outlets open. Recent [[PRED_REDACTION_31]]Rumanian[[/PRED_REDACTION_31]] government-sponsored concerts featuring an American cellist, postponed from last October, received good press coverage.
