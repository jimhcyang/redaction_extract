  Text: Soviet Complaint Over Nasir's Public Attacks on Communists Soviet Deputy Foreign Minister Semenov has complained to UAR Ambassador Kuni that recent statements by the UAR President attacking "international Communism" are unnecessary provocations. Semenov noted that Nasir's remarks would not be pleasing to "high circles"--interpreted by Kuni to mean Premier Khrushchev--and warned that such attacks would stimulate a "reaction" by Moscow. While Semenov did not go into detail over what kind of reaction might follow, it could consist of counterpropaganda pressure by Soviet spokesmen, as in early 1959 when Nasir launched his major anti-Communist campaign. However, Semenov's remarks may have been intended as a hint that further attacks would influence Moscow's future decisions on additional assistance to the UAR or even adversely affect the implementation of bloc aid programs which have already been concluded. Kuni reported that high Soviet officials had expressed satisfaction over the improvement in relations with the UAR. Presumably they were referring to the favorable publicity received by the USSR over the announcement in mid-January of assistance to complete the Aswan Dam project. Soviet officials probably now are especially miffed over Nasir's attacks, inasmuch as they may well have expected that the additional aid for the dam would inhibit Nasir from further attacks on Arab Communists. Since beginning his tour of Syria on 14 February, the UAR President has on several occasions lashed out against Communist activity in the Arab world. He apparently has not heeded the recent warning from Moscow, for in speeches in Damascus on 22 and 23 February he repeatedly denounced "Communist agents" as "enemies of Arab nationalism
