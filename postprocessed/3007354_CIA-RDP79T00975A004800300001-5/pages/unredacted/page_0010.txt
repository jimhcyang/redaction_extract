UAR Deputy Foreign Minister Zeineddine noted that Kuznetsov was anxious to avoid discussion of China's conduct.

China's reservations about Khrushchev's policies and its obvious resentment of the Soviet attitude toward Chinese domestic programs in the past year are currently expressed in Peiping's failure to show enthusiasm for Khrushchev's policy toward the US. The Chinese probably view his reluctance to support them on the Sino-Indian border dispute as a violation of bloc "solidarity." They have not joined in bloc praise for Khrushchev's ability as a theorist and, by contrast, profess to see Mao as the "most outstanding contemporary" revolutionist, statesman, and theoretician, who has "enriched" Communist theory.

Ambassador Thompson believes the Chinese will probably exploit any suitable opportunity to encourage opposition to Khrushchev both within the Soviet Union and within the Communist bloc. They would probably prefer to see Khrushchev replaced, but presumably recognize that his position in the Soviet party is too strong to make secret appeals to other Soviet leaders a fruitful approach. They will try, however, to influence his policies and convince other bloc leaders of the validity of their views.
