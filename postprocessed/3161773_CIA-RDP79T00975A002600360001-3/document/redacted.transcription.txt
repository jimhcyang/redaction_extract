1. SOUTH KOREAN PRESIDENT POSSIBLY ILL

A report from the American air attaché
in South Korea suggests that President
Rhee is ill, and that this fact is being
withheld from even his close associates.
The attaché reports that on 7 July he was
the only person allowed to greet the president prior to flying
him out of Seoul, and that unusual precautions were taken to
shield the president from the view of onlookers. The attaché
states that Rhee's face was badly swollen, and that both his
physical and mental condition appeared poorer than at any
time in the past two years.

3. EXPULSION OF LIBERAL PARTY LEADERS MAY BRING
DISTURBANCES IN HONDURAS

Acting with the approval of his cabinet,
Honduran chief of state Lozano on 9 July
exiled to Guatemala Liberal Party chief
Villeda Morales and two other Liberal
leaders, Francisco Milla Bermudez and
Oscar Flores. This action reportedly
followed Milla Bermudez's attempt to pro-
mote a general strike for 25 July. A
number of student arrests reportedly were ordered after stu-
dents voted to begin a three-day strike on 9 July.

Comment

The Liberal Party, with Villeda as its
presidential candidate, polled 48 percent
of the vote in the 1954 elections. Since May 1956 Villeda's
Communist-influenced faction of the party has been increas-
ingly aggressive in attacks on the government. A threatened
general strike in mid-June was probably promoted by Villeda
and Gonzalo Carias, son of an ex-dictator, who apparently
have a working agreement to oust Lozano.

Key armed forces officials declared their
loyalty to the Lozano government on 13 June.

THE ARAB-ISRAELI SITUATION
(Information as of 1700, 10 July)

Secretary General Hammarskjold has an-
nounced that he will visit Jerusalem on 19-20 July, and Cairo
on 21-22 July.

The American air attaché in Moscow re-
ported on 7 July that 9 sub-
marines, 6 destroyers and 60 coastal and mine vessels are
scheduled for delivery to Egypt by the Soviet bloc.
3 subs, 3 destroyers and 4 coastal mine layers have
already arrived in Egypt. On 10 July a Cairo paper with official
connections stated that Egypt is to receive "several" submarines
from abroad within the next few days. No submarines are known
to have been delivered to Egypt, however, although they are re-
portedly involved in the Soviet bloc arms deal. Egypt is known
to have received 2 destroyers and 4 coastal mine sweepers
from the Soviet bloc.
