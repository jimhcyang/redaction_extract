The success of Razzaz' plans also depends on the achieve-
ment of some accord between the UAR and the pan-Arab Baath-
ist leadership. He said he realizes the UAR will drive a hard
bargain with the Baath, but expressed willingness to subordi-
nate party doctrines, for the time being, in the interest of uni-
fied action. It is not presently known whether Razzaz has dis-
cussed his plans with the leaders of the pan-Arab Baath party
in Beirut.

Razzaz--to whom all the problems involved in such a coup
seem surmountable--believes the United States probably would
continue to provide economic assistance through the UAR to the
new regime he envisages for Jordan. If not, he assumes the
Soviet bloc would provide such assistance.

Organized political parties were banned in Jordan in 1957
after a free election almost resulted in a pro-UAR coup. The
party organizations have persisted despite arrests and other
harassment, and Husayn has even approached them about in-
stalling certain of their members in the government as a means
of increasing support for his regime. Razzaz was among those
approached, and last December he reportedly requested the
Baath party's pan-Arab leadership to approve the participation
of Jordanian Baathists in the government under certain con-
ditions. The central headquarters is said to have rejected the
request, directing the party in Jordan to join instead with
other Jordanian nationalists in forming a "national front" in
opposition to Husayn.
