  Text: II. ASIA-AFRICA Sudanese Coup Attempt Called Off The Sudanese Army faction led by Brigadier Abd al-Rahim Shannan initiated its coup attempt on 22 May, but the plan was called off after movement of forces had already begun. A disagreement between Shannan and other leaders which threatened to result in violence may have caused the decision. The dispute is said to have centered on whether Shannan or a higher ranking officer should become president of the country after completion of the coup. Brigadier Muhyi-al-Din Ahmad Abdullah had proposed that Major General Ahmad Abdullah Hamid, who had just joined the movement, should be offered the presidency. Shannan apparently finally agreed in order to avoid any further dissension. When officers supporting him, fearing a threat to Shannan's position as leader of the group, then swore to kill Hamid and Abdullah, Shannan reportedly decided to call off the coup in order to avoid a blood bath. His decision was too late, however, to stop the movement toward Khartoum of army units from the east and north which were to participate in the coup. As a result, the plot has been at least partially compromised and repressive measures by the government may be forthcoming. Commanders of the two dissident forces, including Shannan's brother, were arrested after their troops arrived outside Khartoum

  Text: Early Release of Jailed Pro-Communist Leaders in Singapore Likely The probability that People's Action party (PAP) Secretary General Lee Kuan Yew will secure the early release of at least six of his jailed pro-Communist party colleagues foreshadows increasingly serious subversive problems for Singapore. He has threatened, if the British refuse to release them, to refuse to form a government after his party's expected victory in the 30 May general elections, which precede internal self-government for Singapore. The Communists undoubtedly believe the release will greatly advance their program to gain complete control of the party and through it the government. For a short time in 1957, the pro-Communists actually controlled the party's central executive committee. Lee has attempted to convince the British that the release of the leaders will have anti-Communist propaganda value because the leading detainee is willing to submit publicly to PAP direction. A more logical explanation, however, is that Lee is under considerable pressure from party extremists on this issue and that he was forced to make the attempt--possibly as part of a deal to obtain extreme left-wing support for the more moderate PAP candidates in next week's elections. Meanwhile, the election campaign appears to be progressing very well for the PAP. The continued disunity and disorganization of the moderate parties when compared with the intensive and apparently effective PAP campaign strongly suggest a PAP election victory with at least a clear majority in the legislative assembly
